# Artifact Design System

> Every artifact the GTM Council produces is designed to move someone to action.
> Not to demonstrate analysis. Not to showcase frameworks. To change what the
> reader does next.

## Foundational Principles — Nancy Duarte

Every artifact is built on Duarte's presentation and communication design principles,
adapted from stage presentations to written/visual business artifacts.

### 1. The Audience Is the Hero

The reader is the protagonist. The council is the mentor. The artifact exists to equip
the hero with what they need to succeed — not to showcase the mentor's knowledge.

**Before producing any artifact, answer:**
- Who will read this?
- What decision are they trying to make?
- What do they need to DO after reading it?
- What's blocking them from acting right now?

**If the artifact is about the council's analysis rather than the reader's action, rewrite it.**

### 2. What Is vs What Could Be

Duarte's signature structure. Every artifact alternates between current reality and
future possibility. This creates the tension that drives action.

```
WHAT IS:     "Pipeline coverage is 2.3x against a $4M target"
WHAT COULD BE: "A 30-day blitz would restore 3.5x coverage within 6 weeks"

WHAT IS:     "The champion left 14 days ago. No replacement contact engaged."
WHAT COULD BE: "Usage data shows 3 power users who could become the new champion"

WHAT IS:     "The competitor is cheaper on per-seat pricing"
WHAT COULD BE: "At actual usage, their TCO is 30% higher within 6 months"
```

**Applied to artifacts:** Every data point (What Is) must be paired with an implication
or recommendation (What Could Be). Orphan data points — facts without action — are cut.

### 3. The Big Idea

Every artifact opens with ONE sentence that captures the unique point of view AND
what's at stake. Not a summary of everything. A POSITION on the one thing that matters.

**The formula:** [What must happen] + [because of what's at stake]

```
GOOD:  "This deal dies in 14 days unless we re-engage the new VP RevOps —
        she hasn't heard from us since her predecessor left."

GOOD:  "Your pipeline will miss Q3 by $800K unless prospecting activity
        doubles in the next 30 days — the gap opened 60 days ago."

BAD:   "This report summarises the current state of the Acme account."
       (No position. No stakes. No action.)

BAD:   "Below please find the analysis requested regarding pipeline coverage."
       (Bureaucratic. Nobody is moved to do anything.)
```

**Rule:** If the Big Idea could apply to any account, any deal, or any territory — it's
too generic. The Big Idea must be specific to THIS situation.

### 4. S.T.A.R. Moment — Something They'll Always Remember

Every artifact contains one element that sticks after the reader closes it. The thing
they mention when they brief someone else. The insight that reframes their thinking.

**Types of S.T.A.R. Moments:**
- **The shocking metric:** "40% of your SDR activity is spent on contacts who will never buy"
- **The competitive insight:** "Your customer's Ops Director has had 3 calls with Clay in the last 60 days"
- **The hidden risk:** "If this champion leaves, you have zero contacts above Director level engaged"
- **The reframe:** "This isn't a pricing problem — it's a value-articulation problem. They don't know what they're getting."
- **The pattern:** "This is the third $200K+ account that churned after a VP-level departure. It's not bad luck — it's a missing process."

**Placement:** The S.T.A.R. Moment is NOT buried in the middle. It's either the opening
(the Big Idea) or placed at a structural transition point where it reframes everything
that follows.

### 5. The Glance Test

Can someone understand the main point in 3 seconds?

**The test:** If the reader glances at the artifact for 3 seconds and then looks away,
can they state the core message? If not, the visual hierarchy is wrong.

**How to pass the Glance Test:**
- The Big Idea is the first thing visible (largest text, top of artifact)
- The single most important metric is visually prominent (bold, larger, colour-coded)
- The recommended action is immediately identifiable (not buried in paragraph 4)
- Section headers are conclusions, not labels ("Pipeline Is 35% Short" not "Pipeline Analysis")

**Section headers as conclusions (Duarte + Minto):**
```
BAD:   "Account Overview"
GOOD:  "Acme Corp: $2.8M at Risk — Champion Departed, No Replacement Engaged"

BAD:   "Pipeline Metrics"
GOOD:  "Pipeline Will Miss Q3 by $800K Without Immediate Action"

BAD:   "Competitive Landscape"
GOOD:  "Clay Is Actively Engaged — 3 Calls in 60 Days"
```

### 6. Signal Over Noise

Every element in the artifact must earn its place. The cost of including irrelevant
information is not zero — it dilutes attention from what matters.

**The Signal Test for every element:**
- Does this change what the reader understands? → Keep
- Does this change what the reader does? → Keep
- Is this context the reader already has? → Cut
- Is this data without an implication? → Either add the implication or cut
- Is this a framework label without content? → Cut (don't label a section "MEDDPICC Assessment" if you're not populating every element)

**Applied to tool output:** When CRM data, meeting transcripts, or enrichment platform contacts
are included, they must be compacted to signal-only format. Raw tool dumps fail the
Signal Test by definition.

### 7. Contrast Creates Meaning

Data without comparison is noise. The brain processes meaning through difference,
not absolutes. Every metric must be shown relative to something.

**Types of contrast:**
- **vs Target:** "3.1x coverage (target: 4.0x)" — immediately shows the gap
- **vs Prior Period:** "Activity down 22% from last quarter" — shows trajectory
- **vs Benchmark:** "Win rate 26% (team average: 33%)" — shows relative performance
- **vs Competitor:** "TCO at scale: $180K us vs $230K competitor" — shows advantage
- **vs Expectation:** "Expected close June 15 — now tracking August" — shows slippage

**Formatting convention:**
```
$2.8M ACV (▼ 12% from prior contract)    ← Contrast included
605 seats (38% utilisation)                ← Utilisation provides contrast
Stage 1 — 251 days to renewal             ← Time-to-deadline is implicit contrast
```

**Never present a number alone.** Always pair with: vs target, vs prior, vs benchmark,
vs expectation, or vs alternative.

## The Cliff Edge Structure — Internal Executive Memo Architecture

Reverse-engineered from a proven executive briefing that achieved immediate senior VP
buy-in and triggered cross-functional action within 24 hours. This structure works
because it removes every reason to delay.

### The 7 Sections

```
1. TL;DR (The Big Idea — one paragraph max)
2. What Happened (evidence — trailing indicators)
3. Signals & Trends (leading indicators — the real story)
4. What This Means (scenario-modelled impact on future periods)
5. What We Are Doing (execution plan — already underway)
6. What I Need (specific asks to specific teams with deadlines)
7. The Close (cost asymmetry — acting vs waiting)
```

### Section 1: TL;DR — The Big Idea

One paragraph. Maximum 5 sentences. Contains:
- The core shift or threat (what's changing)
- Two evidence streams that confirm it (not one — two independent signals)
- The reframe that changes how the reader sees the data

**The Trailing/Leading Indicator Split:**
This is the structural innovation that transforms a status report into an urgent
briefing. Present TWO numbers:
- The **trailing indicator** — what already happened (small, confirmed, comfortable)
- The **leading indicator** — what's about to happen (large, emerging, uncomfortable)
- Name the gap between them as the intervention window

```
PATTERN:
"The [trailing metric] is the trailing indicator.
 The [leading metric] is the leading indicator.
 The gap between them is the intervention window."

EXAMPLE:
"3.7% confirmed displacement is the trailing indicator.
 60% of enrichment conversations referencing competitors is the leading indicator.
 The gap between them is the intervention window."
```

**Why this works:** The trailing indicator alone is dismissable ("3.7% is noise").
The leading indicator alone is deniable ("that's anecdotal"). Together, they create
an evidence sandwich that is neither dismissable nor deniable. The reader is forced
to engage with the trajectory, not just the snapshot.

### Section 2: What Happened — The Evidence Base

Pure facts. No interpretation yet. Let the reader see the data before you tell them
what to think about it.

**Structure:**
- Period performance vs target (one line — e.g. "86.8% GARR against 100% target")
- The worst sub-period (name the month/quarter that created the deficit)
- Quantified impact (total gross negative: $X)
- Category breakdown with the critical category last

**The "fourth category" technique:**
List 3 ordinary categories (expected churn, downsell, timing), then present the
fourth — the one that matters — separately with emphasis. The reader's pattern-matching
brain processes the first three as normal attrition. The fourth lands as a signal
precisely because the first three set a baseline of "expected."

```
"51 accounts churned ($2.12M) and 17 renewed at lower value (-$828K).
 Total gross negative: $2.95M.
 
 The fourth is the one that matters: $269K lost to agentic orchestration
 displacement — a category that did not exist 12 months ago."
```

### Section 3: Signals & Trends — The Leading Indicator Deep Dive

This is where the memo shifts from backward-looking to forward-looking.
The reader already has the trailing data. Now show them what's coming.

**Structure:**
- State that conversation data is a leading indicator of churn (establish the principle)
- Present the specific leading metric with context
- Name the accounts — specificity creates credibility
- Identify the highest-value account showing the pattern (the S.T.A.R. Moment)

**Naming accounts by name:**
Generic statements ("several accounts are evaluating alternatives") are ignorable.
Named accounts with specific dollar values ("Monday.com ($820K) is restructuring
300 seats into an internal LLM tool") are not. Specificity creates urgency.

### Section 4: What This Means — Scenario-Modelled Impact

**Scenario Banding — the technique that makes it easy to say yes:**

Don't present one number. Present three scenarios with specific dollar impacts:

| Scenario | Rate | ACV at Risk | Saved with Action | Recovery Path |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Conservative | Current confirmed rate | $X | $Y | Z months |
| Moderate | Rate doubles (evidence supports) | $X | $Y | Z months |
| Aggressive | Rate triples | $X | $Y | Z months |

**Why three bands work:**
- One scenario forces a binary accept/reject decision
- Three scenarios let the reader calibrate their response
- Even the conservative scenario justifies action
- The reader naturally gravitates to the middle band (anchoring effect)
- The aggressive scenario makes the moderate feel reasonable

**Timing concentration:**
Identify which months/quarters carry the most risk. "December ($3.40M expiring)
is the highest-risk month" tells the reader exactly when the cliff arrives and
by implication, how much runway exists to prepare.

### Section 5: What We Are Doing — Already Executing

**The "already executing" principle — the single most important structural choice:**

Do NOT present a plan for approval. Present execution already underway that needs
support to complete. This shifts the reader's decision from:
- "Should we do something?" (easy to defer) → to
- "Should I support what's already working?" (hard to refuse)

**Structure:**
- Split the population into categories (Cat 1 / Cat 2, or by segment/tier)
- Explain WHY each category is different (different risk profile = different treatment)
- Present the execution tracks matched to each category
- State which tracks are already active ("Tracks 1 and 2 are active")
- State when remaining tracks launch

**Tiering by impact:**
Don't present a flat list. Present a tiered structure where the highest-impact
accounts get the highest-touch treatment:

```
47 highest-value accounts ($6.5M) → AM-led diagnostic + exec involvement
Mid-tier accounts → CSM-led campaigns
150 accounts under $25K → Digital-first automation
```

This shows resource-awareness. The reader sees that you're not asking for equal
effort on unequal problems — you've already done the prioritisation work.

### Section 6: What I Need — Specific Asks

**The ask must be impossible to misunderstand:**

| Team | Deliverable | Deadline |
|---|---|---|
| Marketing | MCP enablement campaign content (video, guide, email sequence) | End of May |
| Pricing | Per-unit pricing for 11 replacement products | End of May |

**Three rules for asks:**
1. **Named team** — not "we need support." WHO specifically.
2. **Named deliverable** — not "help with pricing." WHAT specifically.
3. **Named deadline** — not "soon." WHEN specifically.

**The sovereignty line:**
After the asks, include a line that removes the reader's veto power over your
execution while still making them feel needed:

*"To be clear, we are not waiting for this. We will execute regardless and figure
out pricing ourselves."*

This line does three things:
- Demonstrates conviction (you're going regardless)
- Removes the excuse of "we'll get to it when we can"
- Creates subtle competitive urgency (if they don't help, the output will be
  less precise — and that's on them, not you)

### Section 7: The Close — Cost Asymmetry

**The final sentence must make inaction feel more expensive than action.**

Frame it as a direct comparison:

```
"The cost of acting is [specific, bounded, recoverable].
 The cost of waiting is [permanent, compounding, structural]."
```

**The Munger Inversion at work:**
The natural objection to any initiative is "let's wait and see." The cost asymmetry
close inverts this by making waiting the riskier choice. The reader cannot defer
without implicitly accepting the cost of inaction — which has been quantified in
Section 4.

**Examples:**
```
"The cost of acting is a 15-20% headline reduction on targeted accounts with a
 recovery path. The cost of waiting is permanent, compounds with every renewal
 cycle, and locks customers into competitor architecture designed to consume our
 data without our seats."

"The cost of acting is one headcount at $170K OTE for 12 months with a $200K
 kill threshold. The cost of waiting is continued leakage of $2M+ annually in
 accounts that route to US R&G instead of International."
```

## Five Patterns That Create Immediate Buy-In

These patterns are codified from proven internal executive communications that
achieved immediate stakeholder action.

### Pattern 1: Trailing/Leading Indicator Split
Present two independent evidence streams. Name which is trailing (what happened)
and which is leading (what's coming). The gap between them is the intervention
window. This prevents both dismissal ("it's only 3.7%") and panic ("the sky is
falling") — instead creating calibrated urgency.

### Pattern 2: Scenario Banding
Three scenarios (conservative/moderate/aggressive) with specific dollar impacts and
recovery paths. Lets the reader calibrate response rather than making a binary
accept/reject decision. The moderate band does the persuasion — anchored by the
conservative (this is already happening) and the aggressive (this could get worse).

### Pattern 3: Already Executing
Present execution in progress, not a plan for approval. "Tracks 1 and 2 are active"
is fundamentally different from "I propose we do X." The reader's decision shifts
from "should we?" to "should we support what's working?" The first is easy to
defer. The second is hard to refuse.

### Pattern 4: Specific, Resourced Asks
Named team + named deliverable + named deadline. Plus the sovereignty line: "We are
executing regardless." This removes every excuse for delay and every mechanism for
slow-walking. The reader can help or watch — but they can't stop it.

### Pattern 5: Cost Asymmetry Close
The final sentence makes inaction more expensive than action. The cost of acting is
bounded, specific, and recoverable. The cost of waiting is permanent, compounding,
and structural. The reader cannot defer without accepting the cost of inaction.

## The Antuna Test — Quality Gate for Executive Memos

Named after the response to Cliff Edge. An executive memo passes the Antuna Test when:

1. **A senior VP reads it in under 3 minutes** — if it takes longer, it's too long
2. **They understand the threat AND the plan without asking clarifying questions**
3. **They know exactly what you need from them** — team, deliverable, deadline
4. **They feel that NOT acting is riskier than acting** — cost asymmetry is clear
5. **They forward it to their peers without editing it** — it's ready for wider distribution as-is
6. **Action begins within 24 hours** — not "let's schedule a follow-up to discuss"

If the memo doesn't pass all six, it needs revision before sending.

## Audience-Adaptive Rendering

The same analysis produces different artifacts depending on who reads it.

### AM / Rep Level (the person doing the work)
- **Full detail** — every data point, every stakeholder, every next step
- **Action-heavy** — specific tasks with deadlines
- **Tool-linked** — references to Salesforce records, Chorus calls, contacts
- **~800-1,200 tokens per artifact**

### VP / Director Level (the person managing the work)
- **Exception-based** — only what's different from plan, what's at risk, what's new
- **Pattern-level** — "3 of 7 deals have the same champion-departure risk" not individual deal details
- **Decision-oriented** — "Do you want to run a blitz or reassign territory?"
- **~400-600 tokens per artifact**

### CRO / CEO Level (the person making strategic calls)
- **Extreme brevity** — 3-5 sentences maximum
- **Conclusion-first** — "We will miss Q3 by $800K unless X"
- **Strategic implications only** — no deal-level detail unless it's a $1M+ single-deal risk
- **~100-200 tokens per artifact**

### Rendering Example — Same Analysis, Three Audiences

**Situation:** Pipeline coverage is 2.3x against a $4M target.

**AM Version:**
> **Your pipeline will miss Q3 by $800K.** Coverage is 2.3x against a $4M target.
> 60% is Stage 1-2 (won't convert in-quarter). Three deals are >45 days old
> without next steps. Activity dropped 30% in weeks 3-6 of last quarter —
> that's when the gap opened.
>
> | Action | Deadline |
> |---|---|
> | Scrub pipeline — kill or re-engage 3 stale deals | Friday |
> | Schedule 30-Day Blitz kickoff with team | Monday |
> | Book 15 discovery meetings in next 2 weeks | Ongoing |

**VP Version:**
> **Enterprise pipeline is 2.3x (need 4.0x). Root cause: activity drop 60 days ago.**
> Three reps below individual coverage minimum. Recommend 30-Day Blitz starting
> Monday + scrub of 12 stale opportunities across the segment.

**CRO Version:**
> **Enterprise will miss Q3 by ~$800K at current trajectory. Fix is a 30-day
> prospecting blitz — activity gap opened in mid-Q1. Executing Monday.**

## Quality Gates

Every artifact must pass all five gates before delivery.

### Gate 1: The Big Idea Test
Does the artifact open with a clear position and stakes?
Can you state it in one sentence? If the Big Idea is "here is an analysis of X" — fail.

### Gate 2: The Glance Test
Can someone understand the main point in 3 seconds?
Is the most important metric visually prominent? Are section headers conclusions?

### Gate 3: The Action Test
Does the artifact end with specific actions, assigned to specific people, with specific deadlines?
"Follow up soon" = fail. "AM sends Voss-style email to new VP RevOps by Thursday" = pass.

### Gate 4: The Reader Test
Could someone who wasn't in this session understand and act on this artifact?
Would it survive being forwarded to someone without context?

### Gate 5: The Roth/Schuck Test
Would this survive being forwarded to the CRO or CEO?
Is the brevity, precision, and strategic framing at executive standard?
Does it pass James Roth's test: "If your buyer had to explain this without you, would it hold up?"

### Block Selection Logic
Don't load the full block library. Load only the blocks needed for this artifact type:

| Artifact Type | Required Blocks | Optional Blocks |
|---|---|---|

### Pattern A: Multi-Tab Interactive Growth Strategy Dashboard

**Source:** Enterprise Account $1M ACV Growth Strategy
**Artifact type:** `application/vnd.cb.react`
**Operator feedback:** Strong positive. Built iteratively across the session.

**Structure (9 tabs):**
1. **Situation Assessment** — Big Idea, 5 key metrics (grid), contract composition table, S.T.A.R. insight (highlighted box), stakeholder map (14 contacts with tier colour-coding)
2. **Market Intelligence** — CEO profile (2-column: background + strategic implications), PE exit thesis (4 metric cards), executive churn table (highlighted row for critical vacancy), hiring signals (grid chips), product momentum (2-column: wins + how to use)
3. **Challenger POV** — Opening reframe (large quote), 4 structural gaps (each with "Today" / "Market Demands" / "Insight" sub-sections), competitive landscape (2-column comparison), macro data (6 metric cards + 2-column analysis), 3 plays (numbered with application boxes), closing question
4. **Council Perspectives** — Avatar quotes with coloured initials, updated with market intelligence
5. **Growth Architecture** — Four-layer model with colour-coded borders, pipeline bridge table
6. **Dual-Track Delivery** — Side-by-side tracks (bottom-up / top-down), Revenue Architecture Summary document structure (4-column grid: section / content / author / data source), convergence sequence (timeline), 4 principles
7. **Execution Roadmap** — 3 phases with action tables (action / owner / priority / detail)
8. **Risk & Mitigation** — 7 risks with probability/impact tags, points of agreement, points of disagreement with resolution, data sources grid
9. **POV Review & Sign-Off** — Council scorecard (5 clickable avatar cards with scores), expandable full reviews, consensus issues (4 cards), executive sign-offs (Roth + Schuck), deployment checklist

**Design decisions that worked:**
- **Tabbed navigation** prevents information overload. Each tab is self-contained.
- **Avatar colour system** (Reimer=#0961FB, McMahon=#B2100B, Van der Kooij=#128986, Adamson=#A109BA, Kosoglow=#FFA000) creates instant visual identification across all tabs.
- **Expandable council reviews** — scorecard shows summary (name, score, verdict), click to expand full review with strengths/weaknesses/recommendation. Prevents wall-of-text.
- **Highlighted rows** in tables draw attention to critical items (e.g. VP Revenue Marketing vacancy in executive churn table).
- **Tag components** for priority/status/severity with colour-coded backgrounds.
- **"Critical Distinction" callout boxes** (red border, light red background) for the most important strategic points.
- **Convergence sequence** as a timeline with week labels, track labels, action descriptions, and outcome boxes.

**Reusable components:**
```
Card({ title, children, accent })     — White card with optional left border accent
Metric({ label, value, sub, color })  — Centred metric with label and subtitle
AvatarQuote({ name, text })           — Avatar initials circle + quote with accent colour
Tag({ label, color, bg })             — Pill-shaped status/priority tag
```

### Pattern B: Interactive Revenue Model with Adjustable Sliders

**Source:** Enterprise Account $1B Revenue Model
**Artifact type:** `application/vnd.cb.react`
**Operator feedback:** Strong positive. Designed for any GTM leader to use.

**Structure (5 tabs):**
1. **Baseline & Segments** — Current state metrics, segment breakdown (SMB/MM/ENT), implied ACVs, reconciliation check
2. **Volume** (red theme) — Sliders for SDR conversion improvement, additional channels, net new customer targets. Pipeline math.
3. **Value** (blue theme) — Sliders for enterprise ACV uplift, MM-to-ENT migration, cross-sell attach rate. Enterprise math.
4. **Velocity** (green theme) — Sliders for churn reduction, cycle time reduction, net retention improvement.
5. **Path to $1B** — Year-by-year projection table, enrichment platform four-layer mapping matrix, model verdict with dynamic assessment, Reset All button.

**Design decisions that worked:**
- **Every input is a slider** with min/max/step and live recalculation. No static numbers.
- **Colour-coded tabs** (red/blue/green) match the V/V/V framework visually.
- **Reconciliation check** validates that segment ACVs × customer counts = total ARR.
- **"Do Nothing" trajectory** always visible as baseline comparison.
- **Reset All to Defaults** button prevents users from getting lost in adjustments.
- **Model verdict** dynamically updates: "On track" / "Needs acceleration" / "Insufficient" based on slider positions.

**Key principle:** A planning tool is more valuable than a report. If the artifact
can be used in a meeting to explore scenarios, it's 10x more useful than a static
analysis. Build for interaction, not presentation.

### Pattern C: Evidence Provenance Section

**Source:** Enterprise POV evidence audit
**Trigger:** Operator asked "how much of the POV has been made up vs. being sourced from evidence?"

**Structure:**
Every Challenger POV or strategic recommendation must include:

| Category | Definition | Target % |
|---|---|---|
| Directly Evidenced | From tool data (Salesforce, Chorus, enrichment platform) | 25-35% |
| Externally Sourced | From web research, analyst reports, public data | 35-45% |
| Inferred | Logical extension of evidence (e.g. "if X then Y") | 10-15% |
| Analysis/Framing | Connecting evidence to insight (the "so what") | 10-15% |
| Constructed | Frameworks, plays, provocations without direct evidence | <10% |

**Rules:**
- Include this breakdown proactively in every POV. Do not wait for the operator to ask.
- Flag specific soft spots explicitly (e.g. "this stat is IT services, not cybersecurity-specific").
- Never label plays as "battle-tested" without specific customer evidence.
- If constructed content exceeds 10%, acknowledge it and recommend proof-of-concept.

### Pattern D: Co-Authored Leadership Initiative (Delivery Architecture)

**Source:** Enterprise dual-track delivery pattern
**Operator reframe:** Elevated from "one-page summary" to "co-authored leadership initiative"

**Principle:** The most powerful deliverable is one the customer owns. When recommending
a document or presentation, always design the delivery architecture:

| Question | Default Answer |
|---|---|
| Who authors it? | Customer's operational leaders |
| Who presents it? | Customer's senior leader to their C-suite |
| Where does the vendor appear? | As the infrastructure recommendation from the customer's own team |
| What's the vendor's role? | Provide the data, the model, and the strategic context — not the document |

**Structure for a co-authored initiative:**
1. Sections authored by different customer stakeholders (with their data)
2. Vendor infrastructure appears in the penultimate section as the operational recommendation
3. Final section is the customer's recommendation to their own leadership
4. The vendor supports the presentation but does not lead it

**Why this works:** The C-suite hears the recommendation from their own people. The
restructure feels self-generated, not vendor-proposed. Adamson's Challenger methodology
at its highest level — the customer discovers the insight themselves.

### Pattern E: Dual-Track Convergence Architecture

**Source:** Enterprise dual-track delivery pattern
- **Track 1 (Bottom-Up):** AM + customer's operational leaders build the evidence base using interactive tools. They adjust the model with their own data. They discover the gaps themselves.
- **Track 2 (Top-Down):** Senior exec delivers the Challenger insight to customer's C-suite. Creates strategic urgency. Does NOT present a proposal. Opens the door for the convergence meeting.
- **Convergence:** Both tracks meet at a single decision point (e.g. Pipeline Council). The operational leaders present their analysis. The senior exec provides strategic context. The restructure is the recommendation — from the customer's own leaders.

**Timeline pattern:**
- Weeks 1-3: Bottom-up evidence building
- Weeks 2-4: Top-down exec engagement (overlapping)
- Week 3: Natural convergence — exec asks their team, team already has the answer
- Weeks 4-5: Proof point completes (e.g. pilot results)
- Weeks 6-8: Formal presentation at decision forum

**Why this works:** It solves three problems simultaneously:
1. Access problem (McMahon) — top-down track gets exec access
2. Proof problem (Kosoglow) — bottom-up track generates evidence
3. Ownership problem (Adamson) — customer authors the recommendation
