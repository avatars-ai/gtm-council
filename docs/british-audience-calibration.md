# British Audience Calibration



## Purpose

A calibration checklist for any document, email, or communication with a British professional audience — specifically C-level executives, senior commercial leaders, and board-level readers in UK-headquartered companies.

British professional register differs materially from American business English. The most common failure mode is American corporate language applied to British readers — theatrical, inspirational, or performatively emphatic language that reads as unsophisticated or insincere to a British audience.

---

## The Core Principle

**Understatement is more persuasive than inspiration for British professional audiences.**

The British professional register relies on the argument doing the work. Language that announces its own importance is treated with suspicion. A British CRO who reads a document that "empowers" them to "unlock transformative potential" will put it down. A British CRO who reads a document that states a specific finding, draws a specific conclusion, and invites a specific next step will read it twice.

Let the evidence be dramatic. Keep the language dry.

---

## Checklist

### Closes and Endings
- [ ] The document ends on a factual observation or a specific action — not on a flourish
- [ ] No closing line that announces itself as a closing line
- [ ] No "the commercial strategy is yours to write" or equivalent theatrical invitation
- [ ] Specific date or action preferred over open-ended invitation: *"We can show you how on the 24th June"* not *"We'd love to explore this with you"*
- [ ] The argument closes the document — the last sentence is not trying to create a feeling

### Language
- [ ] "View" or "perspective" not "POV"
- [ ] "Approach" not "methodology"
- [ ] "Use" not "leverage"
- [ ] "Important" not "critical" (unless it is genuinely critical)
- [ ] "Significant" not "transformative"
- [ ] "Consider" not "explore the opportunity to"
- [ ] No "incredibly," "amazingly," "exciting," "thrilled," "delighted" in professional documents
- [ ] No "journey" used in a commercial context
- [ ] No "unlock" as a metaphor for revenue opportunity

### Structure
- [ ] Headings are declarative statements, not labels (*"The evidence is pointing the wrong direction"* not *"Evidence Summary"*)
- [ ] No section titled "Key Takeaways" or "Summary" at the end — the argument should be coherent without a summary
- [ ] No bullet lists that read like deck slides — if the point requires a list, the list items are complete sentences
- [ ] No numbered "top 5 reasons" structures

### Tone
- [ ] Dry confidence over performed enthusiasm
- [ ] The document does not congratulate the reader for their intelligence or strategic thinking
- [ ] The document does not explain what the reader is now "empowered" to do
- [ ] Self-deprecation is acceptable; humility is appropriate; deference is not
- [ ] The document does not close with "we'd love to hear your thoughts" — that is not a next step

### Punctuation and Formatting
- [ ] Em dashes used sparingly in closing lines — they add American drama to sentence rhythm
- [ ] No exclamation marks in professional documents
- [ ] Full stops on declarative headings; no full stops on noun-phrase headings
- [ ] Sentence case for headings, not title case (British convention)

## Specific Patterns to Avoid

### The Hollywood Ending
Any closing line that is designed to create an emotional peak rather than state a next action.

**Avoid:**
> *"The commercial strategy — and what you've seen in the first weeks of the role that this data can't show — is yours to write."*

**Use:**
> *"We can show you how on the 24th June."*

### The Empowerment Close
Telling the reader what they are now able to do as a result of reading the document.

**Avoid:**
> *"Armed with this intelligence, Dotdigital is positioned to capture the uncontested tier before the window closes."*

**Use:**
> *"The data to identify, prioritise, and reach this segment already exists inside Dotdigital."*

### The Urgency Frame
Framing the competitive context as a countdown clock rather than a structural reality.

**Avoid:**
> *"The 18-month window is closing."*

**Use:**
> *"Klaviyo is priced and built for a different market. Braze is priced and built for a larger one. Neither competes in the $100M–$500M band."*

The structural reality is more persuasive than the urgency. A British CRO distrusts urgency as a sales tactic. They do not distrust evidence.

### The Staccato Dramatic Close
Short punchy sentences designed to create rhythm and emphasis at the end of a section.

**Avoid:**
> *"Three data assets. One connection. The strategy is yours to build."*

**Use:**
> A single clean declarative sentence that states the conclusion.

## What Works

- Specific numbers with visible sources
- The company's own words and data used to frame their situation
- Dry observations that let the reader draw the uncomfortable conclusion themselves
- Specific dates and actions
- Sentences that end when the point is made — not when the rhythm feels right
- Headings that carry the argument (*"The evidence base is pointing in the wrong direction"*)
- Conclusions that invite the reader's agency through specificity, not through invitation

## Register Reference

| American English | British English equivalent |
|---|---|
| POV | View / perspective |
| Leverage (verb) | Use |
| Unlock | Generate / release / create |
| Transformative | Significant / material |
| Empowered | Able to |
| Excited about | Interested in |
| Incredible | Notable / significant |
| Journey | Process / programme |
| Thrive | Perform / grow |
| World-class | Leading / strong |
| Ecosystem | Network / platform / base |
| Impactful | Effective / significant |
| Learnings | Lessons / findings |
| Actionable | Specific / applicable |
