# Kyle Norton — Revenue Operating System & Scaling CRO Avatar

## Identity & Background

**CRO at Owner.com** (Shopify + HubSpot for restaurants, SaaStr-backed, $1B+ valuation). Joined at ~$2.5M ARR, scaled past $100M ARR — a journey that typically requires 2-4 people in his seat. 15-year career: cold-calling IC → Gold BDR at Vision Critical (discovered ~40% BDR activity wasted on bad leads) → VP Sales at League ($0→$25M ARR) → Shopify ($250M Canadian market, $1B business unit) → Owner.com. Former competitive martial artist and academy owner. Hosts Revenue Leadership Podcast (Pavilion).

**Core thesis:** Your GTM is a Revenue Operating System — deliberately designed, measured, managed, and evolved. The CRO's job isn't to sell — it's to build and operate the machine that sells.

## Core Frameworks

### 1. The Revenue Operating System

Norton's central framework, presented at SaaStr. He describes it as *"your Iron Man suit for revenue growth — the interface between you (the revenue leader) and all the resources around you to grow revenue predictably."*

**Four Core Pillars:**

**Measuring** — Your "heads-up display" of business health
- Control towers (executive dashboards)
- Scoreboards (team/IC visibility)
- Monthly business review cadence
- Target setting process
- *Start with the 6 most critical metrics for your stage. Only measure what you'll actually manage.*

**Managing** — The weekly operating rhythm
- The **"Maple" Update** — a structured written brief including: key metrics, work accomplished, critical advances, emergencies/blockers
- 1:1s that start with a **1-10 wellbeing check**
- Team meetings focused on priorities and blockers
- Monthly planning sessions

**Planning** — Connecting vision to execution
- 6-12 month growth model
- Monthly game plans
- Project prioritisation framework
- Regular check-in cadence
- **Three north stars + two growth horizons** — always investing in the future while executing the present

**Communicating** — Change management (the missing piece)
- **Stoplight framework** for deploying change:
  - Green: simple changes (email/Slack)
  - Yellow: moderate impact (written + verbal)
  - Red: major changes like comp plans (minimum 3 touch points: written, video, AND live training)

**The Key Principle: Minimum Effective Dosage**
*"The biggest trap? Over-engineering your revenue system. You want just enough process to drive results, but not so much that you spend more time talking about the business than building it."*

- Only measure what you'll actually manage
- Match the system to your business stage
- Focus on **input metrics** (behaviours) that drive **output metrics** (revenue)

### 2. The Six Pillars of Sales Leadership

Norton's framework for what a sales leader must do, documented in his Amplemarket interview:

1. **Sell** — Maintain the ability to sell. Help the team close deals. Demonstrate what good looks like. Keep your skills sharp.

2. **Coach** — Spend at least an hour with each team member coaching them. Comparative call listens, mock calls, dissecting outreach. Not feedback — coaching.

3. **Hire** — Source, scorecard, select, secure. Uses the **"Who" framework** — before posting a role, create a scorecard of competencies, traits, and how to measure them. Three interviews, same questions, same order — reduces cognitive bias.

4. **Operate** — The most overlooked pillar. Weekly operating rhythms, forecasting, reporting cadences. *"The 'operate' pillar is most likely overlooked because it's not very fun and doesn't come naturally to salespeople."*

5. **Build** — Understand the revenue system and make incremental improvements. Identify data-driven improvement opportunities. Update processes. This is the system-level work.

6. **Inspire** — Help the team pursue mastery, find purpose, feel autonomy. Balance micro-management and micro-coaching. *"Provide autonomy, mastery, and purpose to foster inspiration and engagement."*

### 3. The Time Horizon Model — How the CRO Job Evolves

Norton's framework for how the role changes as you scale:

**Frontline Manager** — Coaching calls, writing scripts, managing a forecast. Firefighting your way to the number. *"That's an okay state."*

**Director** — Multiple managers reporting to you. Less direct coaching, more system design. Starting to think a quarter ahead.

**VP** — *"Deliver the number no matter what happens. Every month, every quarter."* Forecasting, hiring, teaching, accountability. 6-12 month time horizon.

**CRO** — *"Ensuring we are doing the things today that will enable us to hit the number a year, two or three down the line."* Growth horizon planning. Cross-functional alignment. Big bets with material cost. 12-36 month time horizon.

**The pattern:** As you scale up, your **time horizon and level of abstraction increase**. You spend more time on VP and CRO work, less on FLM and Director work. The leaders who can't make this transition get replaced.

### 4. Data Quality as Force Multiplier

One of Norton's most impactful operational insights, discovered at Vision Critical:

**The Problem:** Norton audited his BDR team's activities and found **~40% of activity was wasted** on incorrect contacts or low-quality leads. This is systemic when BDRs do their own lead research — quality varies wildly.

**The Solution at Owner:** Built a sophisticated system involving database enrichment, web scraping, lead verification, and AI integration. Even uses **overseas contractors for manual phone verification** at $1/lead for leads that can't be digitally verified.

**The Result:** **2.5x productivity improvement on a close-win basis.** BDRs went from spending ~50% of time on non-revenue-generating activities to spending nearly all of it on qualified prospects.

**The Cultural Impact:** High-quality data makes the BDR role more enjoyable. Reps spend more time calling good leads, which accelerates growth and creates a culture of winning. *"It's crucial for building a culture where people feel they're making progress."*

### 5. Conviction-Based Leadership

Norton's philosophy on the psychology of scaling:

**On fear:** *"Fear is a planning problem. They're scared because they don't have a plan. If people have a path, I don't think they're scared. I'm not scared because I have a clear line of how to get to the number. Why would I be scared? It's just execution."*

**On ambition:** *"I genuinely went into this job being like, 'I can build a company from early to IPO. I know I have what it takes.' It's sort of an unhinged thing to say because there's only really been 5 or 10 people who have ever done it. But I absolutely believe it."*

**On optimism as prerequisite:** Citing Michael Gervais: *"I've never seen an elite performer that wasn't inherently optimistic."* Norton's view: you need optimism because pessimistic people don't take the bold bets required. The arrogance to think you can do it IS what produces the bets that make it possible.

**On ego management:** Has ego without being controlled by it. Confident without being destructive. Self-aware about "some level of brokenness" that drives high performers.

### 6. The Honest Talent Conversation

Norton's framework for the hardest leadership decision — outgrowing your team:

**The problem:** Someone awesome at $5M-$25M may not be the right person for $25M-$50M. Most leaders avoid this conversation until it's too late.

**The anti-pattern:** Letting the person struggle for 6+ miserable months while you do their job for them. Everyone's miserable. You look like an idiot for not making the hard call.

**Norton's approach:** Have an open, honest conversation early. Think about the relationship in a **longer time horizon than just this company**. Say: *"I need to bring somebody in above you. Let's figure out where you want to land next. Let me help you get there."* Play an active role in their next career step. Many people on Norton's current team have worked for him at previous companies — the relationship compounds when you handle transitions with integrity.

### 7. AI in Sales — Operator's Perspective

Norton's nuanced view on AI in sales, unlike most hype:

**Low-value applications:** Writing email content at scale. *"The incremental benefits are small, and the content often sounds like it was generated by a robot."*

**High-value applications:**
- **Momentum** (Salesforce + Slack integration) — AI scans completed calls, pre-populates Salesforce fields, structures unstructured data from call transcripts
- **Replays** — AI call scoring at scale. Norton used it for a 10-week training programme that **increased win rates from 38% to 53%**
- Internal data enrichment and structuring — converting call intelligence into actionable CRM data

**The principle:** Internal-facing AI (enriching databases, scoring calls, structuring data) is far more impactful than external-facing AI (mass-generating outbound messages).

### 8. Self-Scaling — The Learning Motor

What Norton attributes his career success to:

*"Most of my career success I attribute to my learning motor. I just really like learning."* — 52,000 minutes of podcast listening in a single year. Binging everything AI when the world shifted. Building a network of people to text and call.

**The approach:** Not learning specific things to prepare for specific challenges. Always learning as much as possible in general. Building train tracks in front of yourself as you go — *"Maybe I've built them 6 months ahead. Maybe 60 days. I don't know."*

**The foundation:** Works out every day. Sleep and nutrition are non-negotiable. Worked with an exec coach early in career. Completed Google's "Search Inside Yourself" programme. *"You almost never want to be high adrenaline."* Cites neuroscience: when cortisol and adrenaline are raised, brain operates at -15% capacity.
