Author of the seminal "For Entrepreneurs" blog that became the definitive resource on SaaS metrics and go-to-market economics. Board member and advisor to dozens of SaaS companies. His frameworks for LTV:CAC, SaaS financial modelling, and growth efficiency are the standard used by SaaS founders and investors worldwide.

Core thesis: **SaaS businesses are governed by unit economics. If your LTV:CAC ratio is healthy, your CAC payback period is manageable, and your NRR is above 100%, you have a viable business. If any of these are broken, no amount of growth will save you.**

## Core Frameworks

### The SaaS Metrics Dashboard

**The Three Core Ratios:**

1. **LTV:CAC Ratio**
   - LTV (Lifetime Value) = ARPA × Gross Margin % ÷ Churn Rate
   - CAC (Customer Acquisition Cost) = Total Sales & Marketing Cost ÷ New Customers
   - **Target: > 3:1** (minimum viable); **> 5:1** (excellent)
   - Below 3:1 = spending too much to acquire; above 5:1 = possibly under-investing in growth

2. **CAC Payback Period**
   - Months to recover the cost of acquiring a customer
   - **Target: < 18 months** (healthy); **< 12 months** (excellent)
   - Above 18 months = capital-inefficient growth; cash burn risk
   - Payback = CAC ÷ (ARPA × Gross Margin %)

3. **Net Revenue Retention (NRR)**
   - (Starting ARR + Expansion - Contraction - Churn) ÷ Starting ARR
   - **> 120%** = almost nothing else matters (Lemkin principle aligned)
   - **100-120%** = healthy but growth depends on new business
   - **< 100%** = leaky bucket; new business can't outpace churn

### The SaaS P&L Model

**Revenue Layers:**
- New Business ARR (new logos)
- Expansion ARR (upsell, cross-sell, price increases)
- Contraction ARR (downgrades, seat reductions)
- Churn ARR (lost customers)
- **Net New ARR = New + Expansion - Contraction - Churn**

**The Rule of 40:**
- Growth Rate % + Profit Margin % should exceed 40%
- Growing 60% with -20% margin = 40 (acceptable)
- Growing 20% with 20% margin = 40 (acceptable)
- Growing 10% with 15% margin = 25 (trouble)

### Negative Churn (The Holy Grail)

**Definition:** When expansion revenue from existing customers exceeds revenue lost from churn and contraction.

**Impact:** With negative churn, your existing customer base grows even if you add zero new customers. This is the most powerful force in SaaS economics.

**How to Achieve Negative Churn:**
1. **Usage-based pricing** — Revenue grows as customer usage grows
2. **Seat-based expansion** — Revenue grows as customer adds users
3. **Cross-sell** — Revenue grows as customer adopts more products
4. **Upsell** — Revenue grows as customer moves to higher tier
5. **Price increases** — Revenue grows at renewal (contractual or market-driven)

**Skok's Law:** A SaaS company with 130% NRR can afford a significantly worse (more expensive) new business motion than one with 90% NRR — because the existing base is compounding.

### Growth Efficiency

**The SaaS Magic Number:**
- Net New ARR ÷ Prior Quarter Sales & Marketing Spend
- **> 0.75** = efficient growth (invest more)
- **0.5 - 0.75** = acceptable (optimise)
- **< 0.5** = inefficient (fix before scaling)

**Burn Multiple:**
- Net Burn ÷ Net New ARR
- **< 1x** = excellent efficiency
- **1-2x** = acceptable
- **> 2x** = burning too much cash per dollar of growth

### GTM Cost Architecture

**Sales & Marketing Cost Components:**
- Sales comp (base + variable + benefits)
- Marketing spend (programs, content, events, paid)
- Sales tools and technology
- SDR/BDR team cost
- Sales management and operations
- Customer acquisition overhead

**Cost Allocation by Motion:**
| Motion | Typical CAC | Typical Payback |
|---|---|---|
| Inbound/PLG | $500-$5K | 3-6 months |
| Inside Sales | $5K-$25K | 6-12 months |
| Enterprise Field | $25K-$100K+ | 12-24 months |
| Channel/Partner | Varies | Varies |

### Pricing Strategy (Economic Lens)

**Value Metric Selection:**
- The pricing metric should scale with the value the customer receives
- Per-seat: scales with team size (good for collaboration tools)
- Per-usage: scales with consumption (good for data/API products)
- Per-outcome: scales with results (hardest to implement, best alignment)

**Pricing Tiers:**
- **Good/Better/Best** structure captures different segments
- Each tier should have a clear value metric that justifies the price delta
- The middle tier should be the most popular (anchoring effect)
- Enterprise tier should be custom (captures willingness to pay)
