# Objection Library — Jamal Reimer Mega-Deal Framework

## Purpose
Comprehensive response library for objections encountered in enterprise mega-deals ($250K+). Each objection includes the surface statement, the real concern underneath, and the Reimer-aligned response strategy.

## Pricing & Budget Objections

### "Your solution is too expensive"
**Real concern:** They haven't connected price to value, or they're anchoring to a competitor's lower price.
**Response strategy:**
1. Acknowledge: "I understand the investment is significant."
2. Reframe: "Let's look at this relative to the problem cost. You've told me this issue costs $X per year. Our solution costs Y% of that. The question isn't whether this is expensive — it's whether the ROI justifies the investment."
3. Evidence: Share reference customer ROI data.
4. If competitor-driven: "Their price reflects their scope. Let me show you the TCO comparison when you include [integration, support, switching cost, capability gaps]."

### "We don't have budget for this"
**Real concern:** Either it's not a top priority, or the budget holder hasn't been engaged.
**Response strategy:**
1. Diagnose: "Is this a prioritisation issue or an allocation issue?"
2. If prioritisation: "Help me understand where this sits in your top 3 strategic priorities. If it's not top 3, what would need to change for it to be?"
3. If allocation: "Budget follows priority. If we can demonstrate the ROI to [CFO/budget holder], would they be open to a conversation?" → Mountain 2 gap (executive engagement needed)

### "We need to reduce the scope to fit our budget"
**Real concern:** Risk aversion or genuine budget constraint.
**Response strategy:**
1. Validate the instinct: "I understand wanting to manage risk."
2. Warn against under-scoping: "Here's what we've seen: when companies start too small, they don't get enough adoption to prove value. 12 months later, we're having this same conversation with even less budget."
3. Offer phased alternative: "What if we structure this in phases — Phase 1 proves the case at meaningful scale, Phase 2 expands based on results? Same total investment, lower initial commitment."

## Timing Objections

### "The timing isn't right"
**Real concern:** No compelling event, or competing priorities are more urgent.
**Response strategy:**
1. Quantify the cost of waiting: "Every quarter this problem persists costs $X. What's the cost of waiting 6 months?"
2. Identify the trigger: "What would make the timing right? Let's work backward from that."
3. Create urgency: "Your competitor [reference] started 6 months ago and is already seeing [results]. The gap compounds."

### "We want to wait until next fiscal year"
**Real concern:** Budget cycle, or stalling.
**Response strategy:**
1. Understand the real reason: "Is this a budget cycle issue, or is there another concern?"
2. If budget: "What if we structure payment terms to align with your fiscal year? Start now, first payment next FY."
3. If stalling: "What changes in [6 months] that makes this easier? In my experience, waiting doesn't simplify — it compounds."

## Competitive Objections

### "Your competitor offers the same thing for less"
**Real concern:** They've been told they can get equivalent value cheaper.
**Response strategy:**
1. Don't attack the competitor directly: "I respect [competitor]. They're good at [what they're good at]."
2. Differentiate on scope: "The question is whether they solve the full problem. Let me walk you through three areas where the solutions differ fundamentally."
3. Anchor on TCO: "Initial price vs. total cost of ownership are very different numbers. Let me show you the 3-year TCO comparison."
4. References: "Would it help to speak with a customer who evaluated both and chose us? They can share their decision framework."

### "We're already working with [competitor]"
**Real concern:** Switching cost and risk of change.
**Response strategy:**
1. Respect the relationship: "That makes sense. How's it going?"
2. Listen for pain: If they describe any frustration → "Tell me more about that."
3. Quantify the gap: "What would it be worth to your team if [specific gap] were solved?"
4. Lower switching cost: "We handle migration end-to-end. Most customers are live within [X weeks]."

## Scope & Risk Objections

### "We need to start smaller"
**Real concern:** Risk aversion, or lack of executive buy-in for full scope.
**Response strategy:**
1. Understand: "I hear you. What's driving the desire to start smaller — risk management, budget, or something else?"
2. If risk: "What if we design Phase 1 with clear success criteria? If we hit them, Phase 2 is pre-agreed. If we don't, you're not committed."
3. If budget: See budget objection responses above.
4. Warning: "The risk of starting too small is that you don't get enough data to prove the case. That's actually a bigger risk than starting at meaningful scale."

### "What if it doesn't work?"
**Real concern:** Implementation risk, adoption risk, or past vendor failures.
**Response strategy:**
1. Validate: "That's the right question. Let me address it directly."
2. Evidence: "Here's what happened with [reference customer in similar situation]: [specific results]."
3. Structure: "We build in success criteria at [30/60/90 days]. If we're not hitting them, we adjust. You're never locked into something that isn't working."
4. Skin in the game: "We tie [X%] of our fees to hitting those success criteria. We don't get paid unless it works."

### "We tried something like this before and it didn't work"
**Real concern:** Past failure has created scar tissue and scepticism.
**Response strategy:**
1. Respect the experience: "Tell me about that. What happened?"
2. Diagnose: "Was the failure in the technology, the implementation, the adoption, or the vendor relationship?"
3. Differentiate: "Here's specifically what's different about our approach on [the dimension that failed]."
4. Reference: "Would you be open to speaking with [customer] who had a similar experience with a previous vendor and chose to try again with us? They can share what was different."

## Decision Process Objections

### "I need to get approval from [person/committee]"
**Real concern:** They don't have decision authority, or they're not sure they want to champion it.
**Response strategy:**
1. Map the process: "Walk me through the approval process — who's involved, what do they need to see, and what timeline?"
2. Arm them: "Let me build you a one-page business case you can present. What are the three things [approver] cares most about?"
3. Offer support: "Would it help if I/my exec joined that conversation to address technical or strategic questions?"
4. Champion test: If they resist being armed → they may not be a true champion. Reassess.

### "We need more time to evaluate"
**Real concern:** Indecision, information overload, or missing decision criteria.
**Response strategy:**
1. Diagnose: "What specifically needs more evaluation? Let me help you get that information faster."
2. Limit exploration (JOLT aligned): "In my experience, more evaluation rarely changes the answer — it delays it. What would you need to see to make a confident decision this month?"
3. Reduce options: "Let me simplify: here are the two options that make sense for your situation. I'd recommend [option] because [reason]. What questions do you have about that?"
4. Take risk off the table: "What if we structured a [phased approach / satisfaction guarantee / pilot] so the risk of deciding is lower than the risk of waiting?"

## Internal/Political Objections

### "My team prefers [competitor]"
**Real concern:** You've lost the team-level evaluation; need to go higher.
**Response strategy:**
1. Understand: "Help me understand what they prefer about it. Is it specific features, familiarity, or something else?"
2. Assess: Is this a genuine technical preference or a political dynamic?
3. If technical: Address specific gaps. Offer head-to-head POC.
4. If political: Engage executive sponsor on strategic criteria (not just team preference). Mountain 2 play.

### "Our CTO/CFO won't approve this"
**Real concern:** Executive alignment missing (Mountain 2 gap).
**Response strategy:**
1. Map the blocker: "What specifically are their concerns? Is it budget, technology fit, timing, or strategic alignment?"
2. Arm the champion: "Let me help you build the case for [CTO/CFO]. What do they care about most?"
3. Executive engagement: "Would it make sense for our [VP/CxO] to meet with your [CTO/CFO]? Peer-to-peer conversations often address concerns that team-level meetings can't."
4. This is a Mountain 2 problem: The deal architecture may be sound, but political alignment is missing.
